# Breaking the R@200 Barrier in Low-pT Tau 3-Prong Track Finding

The most impactful path to reaching R@200 ≥ 0.80 combines three high-leverage interventions: **physics-informed pairwise features** (charge product + vertex compatibility) in the Stage 2 reranker, **listwise ranking loss** aligned to the R@200 metric, and **iterative refinement** through a second reranking pass on a pruned candidate set. These three changes alone are estimated to close most of the 0.10 gap, with additional gains from physics-anchored attention biases and improved Stage 1 graph construction. The fundamental bottleneck is that the current pipeline treats track finding as a generic classification problem, leaving powerful physics constraints — the ρ(770) mass resonance, opposite-sign charge pairing, shared displaced vertex, and tau mass bound — almost entirely unexploited. Systematically encoding these constraints as features, attention biases, and loss terms transforms a needle-in-haystack problem into a physics-guided search. Below is a detailed technical plan organized by pipeline stage, with estimated impact, implementation complexity, and supporting literature for each proposal.

---

## Stage 1: Escaping the 99.7%-noise graph through physics-aware connectivity

The core pathology of Stage 1 is structural: a kNN graph in (η,φ) with k=16 connects each track to its 16 geometric neighbors, but **72% of ground-truth pions have zero GT neighbors** in this graph. Signal information is diluted by 99.7% noise edges before message passing even begins. The failed attempts (GravNet, EdgeConv, ParticleNeXt, ISAB) all operated within this fundamentally broken graph topology or tried to replace it without sufficient inductive bias. The path forward requires either building a better graph or bypassing graph limitations entirely.

**Multi-space composite graph construction** offers the highest impact-to-effort ratio for Stage 1. Instead of a single kNN in (η,φ), build the union of several physics-motivated subgraphs: kNN in (dxy_sig, dz_sig) space to cluster displaced tracks, opposite-sign charge pairs filtered by distance-of-closest-approach to capture ρ→π⁺π⁻ candidates, and the existing (η,φ) kNN for kinematic locality. This multi-space union ensures that even "uncanny valley" pions — those with low pT and marginal displacement that defeat any single feature space — are connected through at least one physics-relevant channel. Implementation is straightforward: compute multiple kNN graphs with different feature subsets, take their edge union, and feed to the existing message-passing architecture. No architectural changes are needed. The physics argument is direct: signal pions share a displaced vertex (similar dxy, dz), carry opposite charges in pairs, and lie in a kinematic cone — different features that each create a pathway for signal-signal edges. Estimated R@600 improvement: **+2–4%** (from 0.909 toward 0.93–0.95), with corresponding R@200 cascade gains. Complexity: easy, achievable in 1–2 days.

**GraphGPS hybrid layers** address the signal wash-out problem by combining local message passing with global attention in parallel. The GPS architecture (Rampášek et al., NeurIPS 2022) processes each layer through two parallel branches — a local MPNN operating on the graph (capturing kinematic neighborhoods) and a global attention mechanism (allowing the 3 signal pions to attend to each other directly regardless of graph distance). With Performer-style linear attention, the global branch runs in O(N) rather than O(N²), making it tractable at N=1100. This architecture directly addresses why ISAB failed: ISAB compresses all information through a small set of inducing points, which likely loses the rare signal tracks; GPS preserves full node representations in both branches. The global branch creates a "shortcut" for signal-signal information flow that bypasses the noisy kNN graph entirely. Estimated R@600 improvement: **+2–3%**. Complexity: medium, ~1–2 weeks using the well-maintained GraphGPS codebase.

For training under **0.27% class imbalance**, the most promising approach is decoupled training with logit adjustment (Kang et al., ICLR 2020; Menon et al., ICLR 2021). The key insight: data imbalance hurts classifiers but not necessarily representations. Train the GNN backbone with instance-balanced sampling (standard training) to learn good track representations, then freeze the backbone and retrain only the final classification head with class-balanced sampling or logit adjustment (shifting the decision boundary by log(class prior)). This explains why OHEM and ASL failed — they distort representation learning by reweighting during joint training. Decoupled training cleanly separates "learn what tracks look like" from "learn to distinguish signal from noise." Estimated R@200 improvement at Stage 1: **+1–2%**. Complexity: easy, requiring only a two-phase training schedule. A complementary quick win is adding a **virtual node** connected to all 1100 tracks, providing global event context at negligible computational cost — this is trivial in PyTorch Geometric and can help the 9.1% of signals currently missed by Stage 1.

A more ambitious Stage 1 redesign would adopt **EggNet's evolving graph paradigm** (Calafiura et al., 2024): iteratively build kNN graphs in a learned embedding space, perform graph attention, update embeddings, and rebuild graphs for 3–5 iterations. Combined with **object condensation loss** (Kieseler, EPJC 2020) — which learns attractive potentials pulling same-object tracks together and repulsive potentials pushing different objects apart in a latent clustering space — this creates an end-to-end system that jointly learns graph topology and track clustering. On TrackML data, EggNet achieves 93–95% track efficiency. The object condensation β-score directly provides a confidence ranking, and the framework naturally handles the variable-object-count nature of the problem. Estimated R@600 improvement: **+3–5%**, potentially pushing the cascade ceiling above 0.95. Complexity: high, 3–4 weeks, but the ExaTrkX codebase provides a starting point.

## Stage 2: Physics-informed attention and loss alignment unlock the largest gains

The Stage 2 Particle Transformer reranker currently operates on 600 tracks with pairwise Lorentz features (ln kT, ln z, ln ΔR, ln m²) but leaves three of the strongest physics discriminants on the table: charge correlations, vertex compatibility, and explicit resonance mass windows. Adding these is the single highest-impact change available.

**Charge product as a pairwise attention bias** (q_i × q_j ∈ {−1, 0, +1}) is the easiest win. The ρ(770)→π⁺π⁻ decay mandates that the ρ daughter pair carries opposite charges. Currently, the transformer must discover charge correlations implicitly from 16 kinematic features — a difficult task given 0.5% signal fraction. Concatenating charge product as a 5th pairwise feature to the existing interaction encoder requires zero architectural changes: just expand the input from 4 to 5 channels. Builtjes et al. (arXiv:2211.05143) demonstrated that physics-motivated pairwise features in ParT improve background rejection by **10–40%** across jet tagging tasks. Estimated R@200 boost: **+2–4%**. Complexity: trivially easy.

**Vertex compatibility score** provides complementary geometric information. For each track pair, compute the χ² of a simplified vertex fit using helical track parameters and covariance matrices (both available in the 16 input features). Signal pion pairs originate from the same displaced tau vertex, so their vertex χ² will be small; random background pairs will have large χ². Vertex compatibility is the primary handle in traditional secondary vertex finding at CMS and ATLAS, and Smith et al. (Phys. Rev. D 110, 2024) showed that integrating differentiable vertex fitting (NDIVE) into transformer-based flavor taggers measurably improves performance. The computation requires O(N²) = 360K simplified vertex fits per event, which can be precomputed offline using the distance-of-closest-approach approximation rather than full Billoir fits. Estimated R@200 boost: **+3–5%**. Complexity: medium. Together, charge product and vertex compatibility are estimated to provide a **synergistic +5–7%** improvement (to R@200 ~ 0.75–0.77), as they supply the model with information fundamentally absent from the current feature set.

A **ρ mass window soft indicator** — f_ρ(i,j) = exp(−(m_ij − 770)²/2σ²) with σ ≈ 75 MeV — explicitly highlights track pairs near the ρ resonance. While invariant mass is implicitly available via ln m², the Gaussian indicator creates a peaked attention bias centered on the physically meaningful region. This is analogous to the pairwise m/z attention bias used in mass spectrometry transformers, where learned pairwise mass biases improved peptide identification precision by 12.7%. Estimated additional R@200 boost: **+2–3%**. Complexity: easy, as m_ij is already computed.

**Switching from pairwise to listwise ranking loss** directly addresses the mismatch between the training objective (pairwise comparison with 50 sampled negatives) and the evaluation metric (R@200). LambdaRank multiplies pairwise loss gradients by |ΔR@200| — the change in recall from swapping two items' ranks — so training concentrates on the critical boundary near rank 200 rather than wasting gradient on easy pairs deep in the list. For the 3/600 signal ratio, most of the 50 sampled negatives are trivially easy; a listwise loss like LambdaRank, ApproxNDCG, or ListMLE focuses learning exactly where it matters. The LambdaLoss framework (Google, CIKM 2018) and the allRank library provide ready-to-use implementations. Estimated R@200 boost: **+2–4%**. Complexity: easy-medium, primarily a loss function swap with gradient reweighting.

**Iterative refinement through a second reranking pass** is architecturally simple but highly effective. After the first ParT pass on 600 tracks, select the top-200 candidates and run a second (possibly smaller) ParT on just these 200. The signal fraction jumps from 0.5% (3/600) to ~1.5% (3/200), a **3× improvement** in signal-to-noise that allows the second-pass attention to model finer-grained interactions. This cascade-within-a-cascade follows standard information retrieval practice (coarse ranker → fine reranker). The second pass can be trained separately on Stage-1 top-200 outputs, or end-to-end using differentiable top-k selection (Gumbel Top-k or DFTopK). Estimated R@200 boost: **+3–5%**. Complexity: medium.

Supporting improvements include **multi-task auxiliary losses** (predicting charge product, invariant mass, and vertex compatibility from intermediate representations, providing richer gradient signal for all 600 tracks rather than just from the 3 positive labels), **knowledge distillation** from a teacher model with access to truth-level features, and **progressive negative sampling** that shifts from random negatives early in training to hard negatives (highest-scored background tracks) later.

## Novel architectures that reframe the problem

Several emerging approaches recast the tau 3-prong problem in fundamentally different ways that could yield step-change improvements.

**Slot Attention** (Locatello et al., NeurIPS 2020) is perhaps the most naturally aligned architecture for this task. It uses iterative competitive attention to decompose a set of inputs into K "slots," each discovering one coherent object. With K=1 slot (or K=3 for individual pions), the slot learns to attend selectively to the 3 signal tracks through iterative refinement, ignoring the vast background. This reframes track finding from "classify each of 1100 tracks" to "discover the tau object" — a conceptually cleaner formulation for extreme sparsity. Physics priors (mass constraints, charge conservation) can be injected via the slot decoder, which reconstructs physics observables and is penalized when they violate decay kinematics. The Slot Mixture Module (ICLR 2024) extends this with Gaussian mixture representations for more expressive slots. Estimated impact: **+5–10%** if successfully adapted, but this is a higher-variance bet. Complexity: moderate-high, ~3–4 weeks.

**Heterogeneous graphs with physics-typed edges** encode different physics relationships as distinct edge types — vertex compatibility edges, mass-window edges, charge-pairing edges, and kinematic proximity edges — processed by type-specific message passing. The LHCb collaboration recently demonstrated this approach for beauty-hadron reconstruction (arXiv:2504.21844), where a heterogeneous GNN with integrated pruning at every layer substantially outperformed their previous multi-stage GNN framework. The architecture jointly prunes background particles and reconstructs decay chains, directly paralleling the tau 3-prong task. Estimated impact: **+5–8%**. Complexity: moderate, ~3 weeks.

**Masked Particle Modeling** (MPM/MPMv2, arXiv:2401.13537, arXiv:2409.12589) offers self-supervised pre-training on unlabeled track data. Mask a fraction of per-track features and train a transformer to reconstruct them, learning general track-feature correlations from millions of events before any labeled tau data is needed. MPMv2 removes the need for discrete tokenization and uses continuous reconstruction with conditional generative decoders. Pre-trained representations improve downstream performance especially in **low-label regimes**, which effectively describes the 0.27% signal fraction. The OmniJet-α foundation model (SciPost Phys. Core 8, 046, 2025) has already been applied to tau reconstruction, showing 50% improvement in momentum resolution from pre-training. Estimated impact: **+3–5%** when used to initialize the Stage 2 backbone. Complexity: moderate, ~2–3 weeks.

**Physics-informed loss terms** encoding the τ→a₁→ρπ decay chain as soft constraints represent the most efficient way to inject domain knowledge. Adding L_mass = λ₁|m₃π − m_τ|² + λ₂|m₂π − m_ρ|² for the best candidate triplet and dipion pair, plus L_charge = λ₃(|Q_sum| − 1)² for charge conservation, provides training signal that is completely independent of the label imbalance — every candidate triplet can be evaluated against these physics constraints. This is differentiable (invariant mass computation from 4-momenta is a standard operation) and requires no architectural changes. Estimated impact: **+3–5%** as auxiliary loss terms. Complexity: low, ~1 week.

**Signal triplet mix-and-match augmentation** — extracting the 3 signal tracks from one event and embedding them in background from another event — multiplies the effective number of signal training examples by orders of magnitude while maintaining realistic background distributions. This is the simplest way to address event-level imbalance and is especially powerful for the "uncanny valley" regime. Combined with standard physics-aware augmentations (η-φ rotations, pT smearing within resolution), this can provide **+3–5%** improvement. Complexity: low, ~1 week.

## Prioritized implementation roadmap to reach R@200 ≥ 0.80

The following table presents the recommended implementation sequence, ordered by estimated impact-to-effort ratio. Estimated boosts assume stacking with diminishing returns; cumulative projections account for interaction effects.

| Phase | Intervention | Est. Δ R@200 | Complexity | Cumul. R@200 |
|-------|-------------|-------------|------------|-------------|
| **Week 1** | Charge product + vertex compat. pairwise features | +0.05–0.07 | Easy-Med | ~0.75 |
| **Week 1** | ρ mass window soft indicator | +0.02 | Easy | ~0.77 |
| **Week 2** | LambdaRank / listwise loss | +0.02–0.03 | Easy-Med | ~0.79 |
| **Week 2** | Signal triplet mix-and-match augmentation | +0.02 | Easy | ~0.80 |
| **Week 3** | Iterative refinement (2nd pass on top-200) | +0.02–0.03 | Medium | ~0.82 |
| **Week 3** | Multi-space composite graph (Stage 1) | +0.02 | Easy | ~0.83 |
| **Week 4** | Physics-informed auxiliary losses (mass + charge) | +0.01–0.02 | Low-Med | ~0.84 |
| **Week 4** | Decoupled training + logit adjustment (Stage 1) | +0.01 | Easy | ~0.85 |
| **Weeks 5–6** | GraphGPS hybrid layers (Stage 1) | +0.01–0.02 | Medium | ~0.86 |
| **Weeks 5–6** | Slot Attention or Heterogeneous GNN | +0.02–0.04 | Med-High | ~0.87+ |

The first four interventions (Weeks 1–2) are projected to reach the **R@200 ≥ 0.80 target** with cumulative effort of roughly two person-weeks. Phases 3–4 provide margin and robustness. The Stage 1 improvements (multi-space graph, decoupled training, GraphGPS) raise the cascade ceiling, providing headroom for Stage 2 to operate on a cleaner candidate set. The novel architectures (Slot Attention, heterogeneous GNN) are higher-risk, higher-reward bets that could push well beyond the 0.80 target.

## Conclusion: Physics constraints are the underexploited resource

The gap between R@200 = 0.70 and the target of 0.80 is not primarily a model capacity problem — the 2.1M-parameter ParT has ample capacity. The gap stems from **information deficit**: the model lacks direct access to the strongest physics discriminants (charge pairing, vertex compatibility, explicit resonance mass windows) and trains against a loss function misaligned with the evaluation metric. The three highest-impact changes — physics-informed pairwise features, listwise ranking loss, and iterative refinement — are all relatively straightforward engineering tasks that encode known physics or align training to evaluation. The more ambitious directions (Slot Attention for object discovery, heterogeneous physics-typed graphs, self-supervised pre-training) offer paths to R@200 well above 0.80 but require proportionally more development effort. A key architectural insight from the literature is that the **LHCb heterogeneous GNN** with integrated pruning represents the closest existing analog to this problem and should be studied carefully. For the "uncanny valley" regime specifically — low-pT pions with marginal displacement, currently at 24% found rate — vertex compatibility is the critical missing feature, as even small displacements create measurable track-pair intersection signatures when covariance information is properly exploited.
